\section{Sufficiency: A Six-Regime Construction}
\label{sec:sufficiency}

\subsection{The Six Identity-and-Persistence Regimes}

We realize exactly the six identity-and-persistence regimes
derived in Section~\ref{sec:necessity}.
No additional regimes are introduced,
and none of the induced regimes are merged or weakened.

Each regime is implemented with the identity conditions
and admissible-relation profile specified in the derivation.
The construction fixes these regimes as pairwise disjoint,
with extension-invariant persistence criteria,
and without interpretive flags or role-based discriminators.

We now specify a minimal set of typed relations
that connects these regimes while preserving
neutrality and stable reference.


\subsection{Admissible Relations}

A minimal set of typed, directional relations suffices
to connect the six regimes and to satisfy all stated requirements.
Representative relations include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item authority grounding and delegation:
        $\textit{enacts}, \textit{issues} : \text{OBL} \rightarrow \text{NOR}$
  \item participation and party status:
        $\textit{party\text{-}to} : \text{OBL} \rightarrow \text{NOR}$,
        $\textit{involves} : \text{OCC} \rightarrow \text{OBL}$,
        $\textit{has\text{-}patient} : \text{OCC} \rightarrow \text{ENR}$
  \item execution under authority:
        $\textit{occurs\text{-}under} : \text{OCC} \rightarrow \text{NOR}$
  \item applicability:
        $\textit{applies\text{-}in} : \text{NOR} \rightarrow \text{CTX}$
  \item description:
        $\textit{measures} : \text{REC} \rightarrow
        (\text{OBL} \mid \text{ENR} \mid \text{CTX})$
\end{itemize}

% REVISED
Relations in this construction are structural and descriptive.
They assert reference, participation, scoping, or provenance,
but do not encode causal mechanism or normative validity.

Authority grounding and delegation record that an
obligation-bearing entity enacts or issues a normative structure,
without asserting validity or enforceability.

Participation relations record that an occurrence
is associated with enduring referents in recorded participation relations.
The relations \emph{involves} and \emph{has-patient}
denote recorded association only;
they do not assert agency, causal transformation,
or normative responsibility.

The \emph{measures} relation associates a descriptive record
with an enduring referent,
without asserting accuracy, causal relevance,
or evaluative significance.

Occurrences serve as temporal and provenance anchors
for participation and measurement relations.
Descriptive records target enduring referents whose identity
persists across incompatible extensions.

This relation set suffices to satisfy R1--R6
without introducing additional identity regimes,
role-based typing, or extension-layer discriminators.

Temporal and provenance information attach only to OCC and REC
via typed attributes or admissible relations.
These annotations do not introduce new identity criteria
or additional identity-bearing categories.

\subsection{Satisfaction of Structural Requirements}

We briefly verify that the construction satisfies the requirements of
Section~\ref{sec:requirements}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{R1 (Stable identity):}
        Each regime fixes an extension-invariant
        identity-and-persistence criterion.
  \item \textbf{R2 (Obligation-bearing capacity):}
        OBL admits normative attribution
        without reclassification.
  \item \textbf{R3 (Normative reference without execution):}
        NOR grounds authority independently of OCC.
  \item \textbf{R4 (Time-indexed occurrence):}
        OCC captures temporally individuated events.
  \item \textbf{R5 (Applicability and scope):}
        CTX provides first-class scoping referents
        supporting nesting and overlap.
  \item \textbf{R6 (Descriptive indicators):}
        REC represents measurements as stable referents
        distinct from OCC and NOR.
\end{itemize}

No additional identity regimes or extension-layer
discriminators are required.

\subsection{Sufficiency Proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Sufficiency of six regimes]
\label{prop:sufficiency-six}
The six-regime construction specified in this section
satisfies R1--R6 under the assumptions of
Section~\ref{sec:assumptions}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By construction, each of R2--R6 is realized
by a distinct identity-and-persistence regime,
and all regimes satisfy R1.
The regimes are pairwise disjoint,
and their admissible relations introduce no
cross-regime persistence dependencies.
Hence satisfying the requirements jointly
does not generate structural conflict.
No additional regimes are required,
and no interpretive flags or extension-layer
discriminators are introduced.
Non-redundancy of the construction follows because
each of the six regimes realizes a persistence-and-admissibility
profile shown in Lemma~\ref{lem:necessity} to be non-collapsible:
any identification of two regimes in this construction
would instantiate one of the three failure modes
established in Section~\ref{sec:collapse_strategy},
violating either extension-invariant identity or the neutrality constraint.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Tightness of the Bound}

Combining Lemma~\ref{lem:necessity}
and Proposition~\ref{prop:sufficiency-six},
establishes that exactly six identity-and-persistence regimes
are necessary and sufficient
for a neutral accountability substrate
under the stated assumptions.
The lower bound is therefore tight
with respect to the optimization objective
of interoperability under persistent disagreement.
Any substrate realizing fewer regimes must either
embed interpretive commitments at the substrate level,
violating neutrality,
or delegate essential identity-and-persistence distinctions
to extension layers as hidden regimes,
violating the admissibility criterion.
Neither relaxation is available under the stated constraints,
confirming that six is the minimum achievable structure,
not merely a lower bound on an open range.
