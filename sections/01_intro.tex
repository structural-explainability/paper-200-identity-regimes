\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Modern accountability systems increasingly operate under legal,
institutional, and analytic disagreement.
In such settings, a shared data substrate cannot
assume a single interpretation of what records mean,
yet it must still preserve stable reference as records are amended,
branched, refined, decomposed, aggregated, and reclassified.
This paper studies the identity conditions required by that setting.

The analysis treats identity as transformation-invariance:
two referents count as the same only relative
to the transformations that preserve or break their identity.
A minimal accountability inventory supplies the
referents that must remain addressable,
while ordinary provenance and versioning operations
determine which referents can share an identity profile.
The result is a lower bound: under neutral-substrate constraints,
at least nine distinct referential regimes are required.
Collapsing any pair either changes what is
preserved through transformation or
reintroduces the distinction through
hidden roles, flags, or contextual predicates.
The result is not an upper ontology or completeness claim;
it identifies a stability constraint that is often implicit,
and may be violated, in applied ontology and data-governance practice.

Three diagnostic settings make the problem concrete.
In multi-jurisdiction regulatory reporting,
one corporate entity operates under overlapping legal
regimes that individuate obligations,
violations, and responsible parties
differently; audit trails must stay
comparable across jurisdictions without
presupposing which regime governs.
In legislative history tracking,
statutory authority is
enacted, amended, delegated, and contested over time;
reference to the governing rule must
remain stable and independent of the acts that enact or challenge it.
In cross-institutional provenance, a
record is produced, transferred, and
re-analyzed across institutions whose
classificatory and causal assumptions differ,
yet must remain referable as
the same record throughout.

The tempting response is to minimize commitment: represent entities,
occurrences, authorities, and records as one undifferentiated type,
distinguished only by role predicates such as \texttt{isAgent} or
\texttt{isAuthority},
or by contextual flags supplied at query time.
This buys surface parsimony but not neutrality.
It relocates essential
distinctions from the substrate to interpretive mechanisms whose consistent
application requires the shared commitments that persistent disagreement denies.
We call such relocated discriminations \emph{hidden
regimes}: distinctions that are operationally necessary
but not represented at the substrate level.
Under disagreement, a hidden regime cannot be
relied on because its application is not fixed.

This paper describes the kinds of things
the substrate must name,
and what makes two representations the same entity.
Fix the ordinary operations these systems perform,
and identity becomes something to compute
rather than to stipulate.
An accountability substrate must be able to refer
to six kinds of thing: obligation-bearers, rules, occurrences, scopes,
records, and plain referents that are merely acted upon.
That inventory is
not the theorem.
The theorem begins when three of those kinds fail to
determine a single identity criterion.

A jurisdiction can be individuated
by which cases it covers or by how it is
internally organized.
A rule can be individuated by what it requires or by
how its text is structured.
A physical referent can be individuated by its
location or by the object occupying it.
These are not shades of meaning.
In each case, an ordinary operation separates two readings:
decomposing a jurisdiction, refining a rule, or forking a referent.
The two readings land in different equivalence classes,
so they are different identity regimes.
Counting kinds gives six;
counting the identity regimes the substrate must enforce gives nine.

The contribution is this reframing and its consequence.
Identity regime is determined by transformation behavior
over an identity basis, not by ontological kind.
Because three kinds underdetermine their basis,
the number of regimes a neutral accountability substrate
must enforce strictly exceeds the number of kinds it names.
The three surplus regimes answer to no kind on the inventory.
They are forced by operations.
An analysis that stops at naming kinds will undercount.

The result is conditional.
It holds under the neutrality and stability
assumptions fixed in Section~\ref{sec:formal_setting}.
It is a lower bound,
not an upper bound, and it makes no completeness claim:
we do not assert that the operation set is complete,
nor that these nine profiles exhaust the coherent ones.
Adding transformations cannot collapse distinctions
already witnessed by the current basis;
it may leave the bound unchanged or force additional splits.
Structural comparison, intervention, and conformance checking
are left to companion analysis;
this paper addresses only identity.
