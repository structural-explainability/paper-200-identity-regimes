\begin{abstract}
Modern data systems increasingly operate under conditions of persistent
legal, political, and analytic disagreement.
In systems that adopt a neutral substrate, shared interpretation cannot
be assumed, and representations must preserve stable reference across
incompatible extensions.
This paper examines the structural consequences of that architectural choice.
Treating interpretive neutrality and extension-stable identity as explicit
design constraints, the analysis asks what minimal
ontological structure is required for entities and their relations to remain
referable and comparable under persistent disagreement.
Minimality is defined structurally:
reductions are admissible only if they do not
reintroduce identity-relevant distinctions
through hidden roles, flags, or contextual predicates.
A conditional lower bound is established: any ontology intended to
function as a neutral substrate under
persistent disagreement must realize at least
six distinct identity-and-persistence regimes.
A construction with exactly six regimes satisfies the stated
requirements without embedding causal or normative commitments in the
substrate.
Together these results establish a tight structural bound on
entity individuation in neutral substrates.
\end{abstract}

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Preview of Main Result.}
\emph{Under explicit neutrality and stability constraints, attempts to
  simplify ontological structure by collapsing distinctions reintroduce
  those distinctions as hidden interpretive mechanisms.
  Excluding such hidden regimes implies
  six distinct identity-and-persistence regimes
  required for accountability under persistent disagreement:
  fewer do not satisfy the stated requirements,
  and additional regimes introduce no further structural capacity.}

\medskip
\noindent
\emph{Informally.}
Given a neutral substrate capable of supporting persistent
interpretive disagreement, entities and relations
must remain stably referable across incompatible interpretive frameworks.
This requirement constrains how entities may be individuated.
Attempts to simplify the individuation structure by collapsing
categories reintroduce those distinctions as hidden interpretive
mechanisms.
Conversely, a construction with six identity-and-persistence regimes
provides sufficient structure to preserve stable reference without
embedding causal or normative commitments in the substrate.
Together these results establish a tight structural bound of six regimes.
