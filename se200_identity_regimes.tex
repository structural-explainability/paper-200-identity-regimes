\begin{abstract}
Modern data systems increasingly operate under conditions of persistent legal,
political, and analytic disagreement.
Under such conditions, interoperability cannot rely on shared interpretation,
negotiated semantics, or centralized authority.
Instead, representations must function as neutral substrates capable of preserving
stable reference across incompatible extensions.

This paper analyzes the structural constraints
that the requirement of stable reference under persistent disagreement
imposes on ontological design.
Treating interpretive neutrality and extension-stable identity as explicit
design constraints, we ask what minimal
ontological structure is forced if accountability relationships must remain
referable and comparable under disagreement.
Minimality is defined structurally:
reductions are admissible only if they do not
reintroduce identity-relevant distinctions
through hidden roles, flags, or contextual predicates.

The paper establishes a conditional lower bound: any ontology capable of
preserving stable reference under persistent disagreement must realize at least
six distinct identity-and-persistence regimes.
A construction with exactly six regimes is shown to satisfy the stated
requirements without embedding causal or normative commitments in the
substrate.
The result is not a proposal for a universal ontology but a constraint on
ontological design under persistent disagreement.
\end{abstract}

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Statement of Result (Preview).}
\emph{Under explicit neutrality and stability constraints, attempts to
  simplify ontological structure by collapsing distinctions reintroduce
  those distinctions as hidden interpretive mechanisms.
  Excluding such hidden regimes implies
  six distinct identity-and-persistence regimes
  for accountability under persistent disagreement:
  fewer do not satisfy the stated requirements,
  and additional regimes introduce no further structural capacity.}

\medskip
\noindent
Informally: Neutrality is achieved by
externalizing explanatory and evaluative structure to extension layers.
To function as a substrate for disagreement, an ontology must remain
agnostic with respect to causal and normative interpretation.
